# Research — DoneFast Landing Page

Date: 2026-04-25
Method: Competitor copy teardown + negative review mining (Reddit blocked, used adjacent sources) + vertical pain mining + industry pricing benchmarks.

---

## TL;DR — the message buyers respond to

The DoneFast buyer (non-tech operator, 5+ year business, problem-aware via cold outbound or FB ads) believes three things going into this page:

1. **"I need AI in my business but I don't know what to do or who to trust."** This is the #1 internal monologue. Articles call it "AI paralysis." It's not an information problem — it's a confidence-and-trust problem.
2. **"Every AI agency I've talked to is overcharging me."** The phrase "AI automation agency scam" returns thousands of search results. Industry literature confirms it: bait-and-switch, generic deliverables, expensive contracts, intern-level work.
3. **"If I do nothing, my competitors will pull ahead."** Vertical-specific data (esp. home services) shows real, costed losses today — missed calls cost a plumber $50k+/yr.

The page must immediately validate #1, weaponize #2 (yes, most are scams — here's why we're not), and quietly amplify #3.

## Section 2 — Buyer pain & desire mining (non-tech operators wanting AI)

### Verbatim language — "AI paralysis" / overwhelm

- "the biggest challenge isn't adopting AI, it's knowing where to start without feeling overwhelmed" — industry wisdom, [oneclickcontractor](https://oneclickcontractor.com/blog/ai-strategies-for-contractors)
- "Most small business owners feel stuck at square one, overwhelmed by options and uncertain where to begin" — sketchdev.io / Caldwell Chamber
- "AI paralysis" — described phenomenon stemming from: (a) options are overwhelming, (b) terminology is deliberately confusing ("companies label everything 'AI-powered'"), (c) no clear ROI for specific situations
- "The shift is an evolution, not a revolution" — ACHR News (HVAC contractors quote)
- "The contractors getting value usually aren't buying standalone AI tools first" — companycam blog

### Verbatim language — what they wish for

- "We're using AI to stop our estimators from rebuilding field notes every night" — example of what a "good" AI use case sounds like (specific, painful, dollar-clear)
- "Pick one niche… pick one pain with a clear cost (missed calls, slow quotes, messy inbox)… write one simple offer" — guidance to non-tech founders
- "Start at the source: phone calls"
- "Admin tasks that eat the most time without requiring human judgment: reporting, client updates, daily logs, timesheets"

### Synthesized buyer monologue (composite, used as section copy fuel)

> *"I keep hearing about AI everywhere. My competitor just posted about an AI thing they did. I asked an agency and they quoted me eight thousand dollars to build something on Zapier. I tried watching a YouTube tutorial and gave up after twenty minutes. I don't even know if what I want is possible — and I don't want to look stupid asking. I just need someone to tell me what to do, do it, and not rip me off."*

This monologue is the page's job-to-be-done.

## Section 3 — Vertical-specific pain (HVAC, plumbing, home services)

This is the buyer's "if I do nothing" cost. Use sparingly but pointedly — these numbers are unusually concrete.

- "The average plumbing business misses approximately 28% of incoming calls" — suzeeai.com

- "HVAC missed call represents $350–$1,200 in lost revenue per call" — multiple
- "Emergency calls account for 31% of all HVAC calls after hours… $700–$1,050 or more per missed opportunity" — multiple
- "85% of callers who reach a voicemail never call back" — universal stat
- "78% of customers go with whoever responds first" — universal stat
- "Callbacks made within five minutes have a strong recovery rate" — universal stat
- "Contractors typically recover $45,000–$120,000 in previously lost revenue within the first year" — suzeeai.com / numa

### Implication for copy

For non-tech buyers, "AI in your business" is too abstract. Concretize with a single example: missed calls = $50k/yr. This grounds the page so the reader thinks *"oh, that's me."*

## Section 4 — Direct competitor teardown

### SevenLab.ai (closest direct competitor, EU-based)

**Hero:** "AI everywhere in your organisation for a fixed monthly fee"
**Subline:** "Production-ready AI shipped by a dedicated team — without scope creep, sprint chaos, or hidden costs"
**Pricing:**
- AI Agents — €5,500/mo (paused: €1,375/mo, 75% reduction)
- AI Applications — €9,500/mo
- Enterprise — Custom
- Sovereign EU AI — €3,500/mo
- 7-Day PoC — €5,000 fixed
**Hooks:** "Pause whenever you want" / "No minimum commitment, no lock-in" / "Unlimited projects (one at a time)"
**Differentiation gap for DoneFast:** SevenLab targets enterprise/EU, charges 4-5x DoneFast's price (€5,500 ≈ $6,000), no clear refund guarantee, language is technical ("sovereign infrastructure", "AI applications"). DoneFast can win on (1) price, (2) plain-English copy for non-tech buyers, (3) the 75% refund.

### DesignDelulu.com (referenced, page failed to fetch — based on Google snippet)

**Positioning:** "AI Automation Agency | Unlimited Design & Dev"
**Model:** Flat monthly fee, unlimited design + dev requests, pause/cancel anytime
**Differentiation gap:** Includes design — different bundle. DoneFast is implementation-only, faster, cheaper. Don't try to compete on design.

### Industry pricing benchmarks (use as "anchor against the market" line)

- $1,500–$15,000 per project — typical AAA standalone project
- $2,000–$20,000+/mo — typical AAA monthly retainer (avg $3,200/mo)
- $99–$500/mo — basic chatbot / email trigger (low end)
- $1,000–$5,000+/mo — enterprise AI personalization / orchestration
- $5K–$50K+ — AI strategic consulting engagements
- $5M–$50M — enterprise AI transformations (18-36 months)

### Anchor sentence (for pricing section)

> *"Industry average for one automation: $3,200 per month. We're $1,250 — flat. And we don't ship a strategy deck."*

## Section 5 — The category-defining objection map

Six recurring objections in this category. Each gets handled on the page, ideally as a one-liner:

| # | Objection (in buyer's voice) | Page handle |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Aren't all of these just scams?" | Lead with category critique. Quote the stats. Position DoneFast as the deliberate non-scam version. |
| 2 | "Will I get generic ChatGPT-wrapper slop?" | Senior engineer review on every build. Plain-English description of what we actually build. |
| 3 | "What if I get locked in?" | $1,250/mo flat, pause/cancel anytime, 75% refund first week. Repeat in 3 places minimum. |
| 4 | "I don't even know what to ask for." | Lead with examples of common builds (missed-call automation, quote follow-up, lead intake) so buyer can self-identify. |
| 5 | "Is this guy actually qualified?" | Founder origin: "AI nerd for years, world is catching up." Show worked-with logos. |
| 6 | "What if AI moves too fast and what you build is obsolete?" | Faster shipping cycles than the change cycle: 3-5 day delivery. |

## Section 6 — Big idea / positioning bet

**Wiebe's "big idea" framework:** the most powerful conversion driver is a single, contrarian-feeling positioning statement that reframes the buyer's problem.

**Candidate big ideas (ranked):**

1. **"Most AI agencies are running the most expensive scam in business history. We're the alternative."** — strongest. Uses buyer's existing belief. Hijacks the negative review consensus.

2. **"You don't need an AI strategy. You need three things shipped."** — second strongest. Reframes the consulting trap.

3. **"You already know you need AI. You just don't know who to trust."** — softer, more empathetic, better for FB ad-cold traffic.

**Recommendation:** lead with **#3** (empathetic) for the hero, then deploy **#1** (aggressive) in the section right after the trusted-by logos to handle the "is this a scam too?" objection, then deploy **#2** (reframe) in the pricing section.

This sequence works for problem-aware traffic from cold outbound and FB ads — it meets them where they are, then escalates.

## Section 7 — Voice & language patterns to use vs. avoid

### Use (mined from positive sources)
- "Ship" (verb) — high-signal across builder language
- "Working" — as in "working AI", "working automation"
- "Plain English"
- "First thing", "first build", "first week"
- Specific dollar numbers, specific timelines (3-5 days, $1,250)
- "We tell you what to build" — handles the "I don't know what to ask for" objection
- "Cancel" / "pause" / "refund" — the de-risk vocabulary

### Avoid (mined from agency copy that triggers buyer skepticism)
- "Transform your business"
- "Unlock the power of AI"
- "Revolutionize"
- "Synergy", "leverage", "supercharge"
- "Sovereign infrastructure" / "agentic" / "model orchestration" — anything technical the non-tech buyer won't parse
- "Roadmap", "strategy", "framework", "assessment" — these are scam-coded for our buyer
- "Schedule a discovery call" — too consulting-coded; use "book a call"

## Section 8 — Founder origin angle (Pete)

Pete's quote (from discovery): *"I started DoneFast because I'm an AI nerd. I've been going down this rabbit hole for years. Now the world's catching up — every business owner knows they need AI in their business — but they haven't spent the last few years living inside it like I have."*

This is gold. It does three things at once:
1. Establishes credibility (years of head start)
2. Empathizes with the buyer ("you know you need it")
3. Implicitly trashes the "AI consultant who hasn't shipped" competitor

**Use directly as quoted block on page.** Don't sanitize. The slight informality ("AI nerd", "rabbit hole") is the feature, not a bug — it's what makes Pete sound real to a non-tech buyer who's been talked down to by McKinsey-style consultants.
